## Purpose

Document the Zootechnical Description of the assessment scope by mapping the
target animal population into the organizing levels required by Module I:
**Species**, **Production System**, **Life-Fates**, **Life-Phases**, and the
**Circumstances** present within each Life-Phase.

Module I produces the structured description of the living conditions and
processes that form the causal starting point of the WFF chain:

`Circumstances → Biological Consequences → Affective Experiences → Welfare Metrics`

It does not itself estimate Biological Consequences, Affective Experiences, or
welfare outcomes; it defines the system context and Circumstances that
Module II evaluates.

## Core entities

> Authoritative WFF definitions and resolved methodological decisions are
> maintained in `canon/`, `glossary/`, the relevant canon files, and
> `review/resolved_methodological_decisions.md`. This section identifies the
> entities used by this module and their module-specific role; it is not an
> independent glossary.

Module I is organized hierarchically across five levels. Lower levels are
nested within those above.

### 1. Species

Canonical WFF entity. **Module I role:** highest-level biological scope of
the Zootechnical Description. Include relevant breed, strain, line, genotype,
sex, age, or developmental class where these affect exposure, vulnerability,
or interpretation of Circumstances.

### 2. Production System

Canonical framework entity. **Module I role:** organizing context within
which Life-Fates, Life-Phases, and Circumstances are described — for example:
intensive indoor system, pasture-based system, cage-free aviary, net-pen
aquaculture system, laboratory setting, zoo, sanctuary, companion-animal
context, or wild setting.

The Production System is an organizing level above Circumstances and must
not be treated as merely one Circumstance among others.

### 3. Life-Fates

Canonical WFF entity. **Module I role:** separates groups of individuals
with meaningfully different life trajectories, exposures, roles, or
endpoints. Examples may include: market animals, breeding females, breeding
males, culled animals (e.g., day-old males), animals dying before slaughter,
replacement animals, or ancillary species involved in the production chain.

Life-Fates within the same Production System should be assessed separately
when their Circumstances, Life-Phases, Biological Consequences, or Affective
Experiences differ in welfare-relevant ways.

Life-Fates are pragmatic analytical partitions, not exhaustive taxonomies of
all possible animal trajectories within a system. First-pass analyses should
prioritise the numerically significant and welfare-relevant portions of the
population. Residual animals or pathways not yet represented by the current
partition should be recorded explicitly — with estimated percentage or count
where available, or marked as unknown for later refinement — rather than
silently omitted.

### 4. Life-Phases

Canonical WFF entity. **Module I role:** temporal organization of
Circumstances within each Life-Fate. Examples include: hatching, suckling,
early rearing, grow-out, breeding cycle, transport, lairage, slaughter, or
other system-specific phases. Life-Phases are defined separately for each
Life-Fate and provide the temporal structure for documenting Circumstances
and identifying Biological Consequences.

### 5. Circumstances

Canonical WFF entity (see `canon/terminology.md`, Circumstance entry).
**Module I role:** internal and external conditions documented within each
Life-Phase of each Life-Fate, then passed to Module II.

**External Circumstances** include physical, social, environmental, procedural,
and management-related conditions:

- Housing type (e.g., cage type, floor type, indoor/outdoor access)
- Stocking density and space allowance
- Feed and water provisioning (access, quality, restriction regimes)
- Environmental enrichment (presence, type, accessibility)
- Light, air quality, and climate conditions
- Social structure (group size, mixing, social stability)
- Husbandry practices (handling, procedures, routine interventions)

**Internal Circumstances** include animal attributes that shape vulnerability,
response, or exposure:

- Breed and line
- Genotype
- Sex
- Age and developmental status
- Genetic predispositions (e.g., to metabolic disorders or rapid growth)

## Inputs

- Farm management records and industry standards
- Published husbandry and production-system literature
- Regulatory and certification definitions of production systems
- Species-Specific Needs assessments relevant to the system and Life-Fates
- Population data (numbers or proportions of animals per Life-Fate, mortality
  rates, replacement rates, flock or herd turnover, and phase-duration records)
  needed to construct or audit the welfare-burden numerator
- Commercial output data (commercial yields and product outputs, such as eggs
  per year, kg liveweight sold, kg carcass, kg edible meat, litres milk) needed
  for denominator selection in later Module V standardisation
- Expert knowledge of system-specific conditions

---

## Outputs

A structured Zootechnical Description of the target population and system,
formatted for use as input to Module II (Veterinary Inventory). Outputs should
specify:

- Species and relevant biological attributes (breed, sex, production class, etc.)
- Production System or scenario assessed
- Life-Fates included and any explicitly excluded from the assessment
- Life-Phases defined for each Life-Fate
- Circumstances documented within each Life-Phase (distinguishing External and
  Internal Circumstances)
- Species-Specific Needs relevant to interpreting those Circumstances
- **Population accounting** for each Life-Fate: the numbers or proportions
  of animals represented and, where needed, the numbers or fractions moving
  through Life-Phases. Residual animals or pathways not represented by the
  current Life-Fate partition should be recorded explicitly — with estimated
  percentage or count where available, or marked as unknown for later
  refinement. Population accounting supports construction of the cumulative
  welfare-burden numerator.
- **Productivity Metrics**: commercial output-denominator candidates for
  later Module V standardisation, such as eggs per year, kg liveweight sold,
  kg carcass, kg edible meat, kg retail cuts, litres milk, or kg processed
  product where processing is inside the analytical boundary. Module I records
  candidate denominators; Module V determines allocation rules and performs
  standardisation.
- Geographical, temporal, and system boundaries of the assessment
- Which descriptions are well-documented, estimated, assumed, or variable across
  the system
- Data sources and dates of information

## Required distinctions

- **Circumstances ≠ Biological Consequences.** Module I stops at Circumstances.
  Do not carry biological outcomes or welfare inferences into this module.
- **Production System ≠ a Circumstance.** The Production System is the
  organizing context within which Life-Fates, Life-Phases, and Circumstances
  are described. It is not a Circumstance item within External Circumstances.
- **Life-Fates ≠ Life-Phases.** Life-Fates are distinct classes of individuals;
  Life-Phases are temporal stages within a Life-Fate.
- **Population accounting ≠ Productivity Metrics.** Population accounting
  (numbers or proportions of animals per Life-Fate, movement through
  Life-Phases, mortality, replacement rates) supports the welfare-burden
  numerator. Productivity Metrics (commercial output denominators such as kg
  liveweight sold, kg carcass, kg edible meat) support Module V
  standardisation. They serve different calculation roles and must not be
  conflated.
- **Documented vs. assumed conditions.** Distinguish what is directly measured
  or reported from what is inferred or assumed.
- **System-level vs. individual-level.** Clearly specify the level of
  description.

## Common failure modes

- Conflating a Circumstance (e.g., "barren housing") with a welfare outcome
  (e.g., "stereotypic behaviour") — outcome belongs in Module II.
- Treating the Production System as one bullet under External Circumstances,
  obscuring the hierarchical organization of the description.
- Omitting Life-Fates that have meaningfully different welfare-relevant
  Circumstances or trajectories within the Production System.
- Collapsing all Life-Phases into a single undifferentiated description.
- Under-specifying Internal Circumstances such as breed, genotype, or
  developmental status when these affect vulnerability or exposure.
- Under-specifying conditions that vary substantially within a system type.
- Over-specifying conditions for which reliable data do not exist.
- Using industry marketing categories as if they were standardised welfare
  descriptors.
