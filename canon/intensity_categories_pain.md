## 1. Conceptual overview

This file provides the operational definitions of the WFF Pain intensity categories. It complements `canon/terminology.md`, which establishes the surrounding terms (Pain, Pain Intensity, Affective State, Pain-Track, Cumulative Pain) but defers the full per-category definitions to this file.

In the WFF, **Pain** is used as the umbrella term for any felt negative affective state, whether of physical origin (e.g., injury, hunger, heat stress) or psychological origin (e.g., fear, anxiety, frustration). The capitalized form distinguishes this technical usage from the everyday sense of the word.

**Pain Intensity** denotes the subjective degree of unpleasantness, aversiveness, disruption, or attention capture experienced by an individual. It is **not** inferred directly from the magnitude of a physical stimulus or the severity of an underlying Biological Consequence; it is a hypothesis about the felt severity of the experience itself.

Pain intensity categories are **assigned to temporal segments or episodes within an Affective Experience, not globally to an animal, a condition, or a Biological Consequence**. The same condition may give rise to different intensities at different segments of its temporal course, in different individuals, or under different modulating circumstances.

Pain intensity categories are **hypotheses about affective experience inferred from converging streams of evidence** — behavioral, physiological, neurological, pharmacological, anatomical, evolutionary, and functional. They are revisable in the light of new evidence.

## 2. Methodological principles

The following principles govern the use of Pain intensity categories throughout the WFF and apply to every per-category definition below.

1. **Subjective, not stimulus-based.** Intensity refers to the felt experience, not to the magnitude of the stimulus or the severity of the underlying Biological Consequence.
2. **Segment-level, not global.** Categories are assigned to temporal segments of Affective Experiences. An individual is not classified as "an Annoying Pain animal"; a segment of an Affective Experience is classified as Annoying Pain.
3. **Hypothesis-based, evidence-informed.** Each intensity assignment in the WFF is an explicit inferential hypothesis about the most plausible affective intensity associated with an Affective Experience, grounded in converging welfare indicators such as behavioral, physiological, neurological, pharmacological, anatomical, evolutionary, and functional evidence.

   Because Affective Experiences cannot be directly observed, the framework does not treat intensity assignment as a deterministic classification. Instead, available evidence is used to estimate the relative plausibility that an experience falls within different intensity categories. Stronger, more consistent, and more directly relevant evidence should reduce uncertainty, typically concentrating estimates within one or a narrower set of adjacent intensity categories. Weaker, conflicting, indirect, or sparse evidence should produce broader plausibility distributions across categories, with assumptions and uncertainty made explicit.

   This approach is consistent with the WFF distinction between welfare indicators, which provide indirect evidence about affective states, and welfare metrics, which integrate such evidence into structured estimates of time spent in Pain or Pleasure.
4. **Disaggregated.** Cumulative Pain remains disaggregated by intensity category. Time spent in Annoying, Hurtful, Disabling, and Excruciating Pain is reported separately. Categories are not collapsed.
5. **No numeric weights are defined in this file.** This file does not assign numerical weights, ratios, or equivalences between intensity categories. Any such weighting belongs to optional analytical layers and must be documented with explicit assumptions where used.
6. **No cross-category equivalence is implied.** Adjacent categories are qualitatively distinct. The category ordering reflects increasing severity but does not imply equal spacing or arithmetic relationships between adjacent levels.
7. **No cross-valence comparison here.** Pain intensity categories are not compared with Pleasure intensity categories in this file. Pain and Pleasure are valence-specific and are not assumed to sit on a common scale (see `canon/terminology.md`).
8. **Absence of overt expression is not absence of Pain.** Three constructs must remain distinct: (a) the **inferred affective intensity** of the felt experience, (b) the **functional disruption** observable in behavior and cognition, and (c) the **overt expression of Pain** through vocalizations, postures, facial expressions, or similar signals. Overt expression is one stream of evidence among many; its absence does not establish low intensity, particularly in prey species, in concealment-adapted species, in early-life stages, or in contexts where expression is suppressed.

## 3. Canonical categories

The WFF Pain intensity categories, in canonical order, are:

1. **No Pain**
2. **Annoying**
3. **Hurtful**
4. **Disabling**
5. **Excruciating**

**No Pain** is not an active negative Pain intensity category and does not contribute to Cumulative Pain totals. It denotes periods in which no negative affective state categorized as Pain is hypothesized to be experienced. It remains an explicit state within Pain-Track segmentation and temporal accounting, so that segments without Pain are represented explicitly rather than implicitly.

The four active negative intensity categories, in order of increasing severity, are: **Annoying → Hurtful → Disabling → Excruciating**.

---

## 4. No Pain

**Definition.** A temporal segment in which no negative affective state categorized as Pain is hypothesized to be experienced.

**Role in Pain-Track segmentation.** No Pain is a legitimate state within the Pain-Track state-space. Segments classified as No Pain are explicitly represented in temporal accounting so that the absence of negative affect during a portion of an Affective Experience is recorded rather than left implicit.

**Role in Cumulative Pain.** No Pain segments do not contribute to time in Annoying, Hurtful, Disabling, or Excruciating Pain. They are tracked separately for completeness of temporal accounting and to support Sensitivity Analysis and Traceability.

**Relationship to Pleasure.** A segment classified as No Pain is silent only about Pain. Whether the same segment contains positive Affective Experience is a separate determination made within the Pleasure-Track and Cumulative Pleasure constructs.

**Source basis.** Derived from `canon/terminology.md` (Pain, Pain Intensity, Affective State entries) and the underlying methodological sources `wfi-technical-definitions` and `wff-methodological-foundations-2025`. The `wfi-pain-intensities` source defines only the four active categories.

## 5. Annoying

**Summary.** Pain perceived as aversive but not intense enough to disrupt routine in a way that alters adaptive functioning or that affects the behaviors the individual is motivated to perform.

**Operational descriptors (from `wfi-pain-intensities`):**

- **Disruption of routine activities:** none.
- **Performance of motivated and positive behaviors:** not affected. Annoying Pain should not deter individuals from enjoying pleasant experiences with no short-term function (e.g., play) or from positive social interactions.
- **Willingness to trade resources, work, or safety for Pain relief:** minimal.
- **Pain-specific behavioral manifestations** (vocalizations, shaking, muscle tension, specific motor patterns, facial expressions): not expected.
- **Attention to Pain — can the sensation be ignored?** Yes, most of the time.
- **Attention to surroundings:** not affected.
- **Cognitively demanding tasks:** individuals can focus, solve problems, and remain attentive; the discomfort can be ignored most of the time. Performance is either not affected or only mildly affected.
- **Neurophysiological departures from baseline:** no measurable departures expected.
- **Effectiveness of Pain-relieving drugs** (analgesics for physical Pain, psychotropics for psychological Pain): no effect expected.
- **Tolerance:** can be tolerated continuously.

**Distinguishing character.** Pain is felt but does not capture attention or alter motivated behavior. The individual can engage in activities with no immediate short-term benefit (e.g., play) without those activities being deterred by the Pain.

---

## 6. Hurtful

**Summary.** Pain intensity that clouds focus but allows basic tasks. Hurtful Pain disrupts the ability of individuals to function optimally without preventing routine activity.

**Operational descriptors (from `wfi-pain-intensities`):**

- **Disruption of routine activities:** to some degree. Routine activities important in the short term (e.g., eating, foraging) can still be conducted, but with impairment in ability or motivation.
- **Performance of motivated and positive behaviors:** frequency and/or duration reduced. Engagement in positive activities with no immediate benefits is not expected.
- **Willingness to trade resources, work, or safety for Pain relief:** moderate willingness to trade resources; risk-taking behavior not affected.
- **Pain-specific behavioral manifestations:** not expected, particularly in prey species.
- **Attention to Pain — can the sensation be ignored?** For some time, depending on the level of distraction provided by other activities. Awareness of Pain is likely present most of the time, interspersed with brief periods during which Pain can be ignored.
- **Attention to surroundings:** reduced to some degree. Reduced alertness and inattention to ongoing stimuli may be present.
- **Cognitively demanding tasks:** can still be performed, but attention and motivation are reduced; performance drops; Pain often intrudes on concentration.
- **Neurophysiological departures from baseline:** mild departures expected.
- **Effectiveness of Pain-relieving drugs:** typical doses are expected to be effective at eliminating the Pain.
- **Tolerance:** can be tolerated continuously.

**Distinguishing character.** Pain is now sufficient to reduce the engagement, duration, or frequency of motivated behaviors and to intrude on attention, but not yet sufficient to take priority over most behavioral bids. Drug responsiveness at typical doses begins to be an expected feature.

---

## 7. Disabling

**Summary.** Pain that takes priority over most bids for behavioral execution and prevents most forms of enjoyment or positive welfare. Pain is continuously distressing.

**Operational descriptors (from `wfi-pain-intensities`):**

- **Disruption of routine activities:** substantial. Affected individuals often change their activity levels drastically.
- **Performance of motivated and positive behaviors:** not expected.
- **Willingness to trade resources, work, or safety for Pain relief:** willingness to trade important resources, work, and safety to avoid Pain.
- **Pain-specific behavioral manifestations:** likely.
- **Attention to Pain — can the sensation be ignored?** Very briefly; Pain is continuous. Inattention and unresponsiveness to milder forms of Pain or to other ongoing stimuli and surroundings is likely.
- **Attention to surroundings:** substantially reduced.
- **Cognitively demanding tasks:** focus is continuously hijacked by Pain; most higher-level cognitive processes and learning tasks are disrupted.
- **Neurophysiological departures from baseline:** marked.
- **Effectiveness of Pain-relieving drugs:** only higher doses or more powerful drugs provide relief.
- **Tolerance:** barely tolerated, with major impairment of life quality.

**Critical inferential caveat (source-flagged).** The degree of disruption in an organism's ability to function optimally must **not** be confused with the overt expression of Pain behaviors. Overt expression is less likely in prey species. A Disabling-level intensity may be present even when conspicuous behavioral expression is muted, absent, or masked. The three constructs — inferred affective intensity, functional disruption, and overt expression — must be evaluated separately and triangulated, not collapsed.

**Terminological note.** "Disabling" refers to the disability caused by the Pain itself, not to any pre-existing structural disability of the individual.

---

## 8. Excruciating

**Summary.** Extreme levels of Pain that are not normally tolerated, even for short durations.

**Operational descriptors (from `wfi-pain-intensities`):**

- **Disruption of routine activities:** complete.
- **Performance of motivated and positive behaviors:** impossible.
- **Willingness to trade resources, work, or safety for Pain relief:** complete focus on eliminating Pain at all costs, including sacrifice of vital resources.
- **Pain-specific behavioral manifestations:** extreme. May include loud screaming, involuntary shaking, extreme muscle tension, or extreme restlessness.
- **Attention to Pain — can the sensation be ignored?** No.
- **Attention to surroundings:** attention is exclusive to Pain.
- **Cognitively demanding tasks:** cognitive functioning collapses; the mind is consumed by the experience of Pain itself, leaving almost no room for attention to other tasks.
- **Neurophysiological departures from baseline:** extreme.
- **Effectiveness of Pain-relieving drugs:** anesthesia or unconsciousness may be required for relief.
- **Tolerance:** can be willingly tolerated for only seconds.

**Source-derived analytical criteria.** Two additional criteria are supplied by the source:

1. **Conditions and events associated with extreme Pain not normally tolerated even for a few seconds.** The source gives scalding and severe burning events as examples of conditions whose intensity profile is taken to reach this level.
2. **Manifestation of behaviors that the individual would strongly refrain from displaying under normal circumstances**, because such behaviors threaten body integrity. Examples include running into hazardous areas or exposing oneself to sources of danger (such as predators) as a result of the Pain or of attempts to alleviate it.

The source also notes that **concealment of Pain is not possible** at this level. Accordingly, when expression is physically and situationally possible, overt expression of Pain is generally expected at Excruciating intensity. This does not, however, invert the inferential safeguard of §2.8: the absence of extreme overt expression must not by itself rule out Excruciating Pain, because expression may be limited by physiological collapse, restraint, paralysis, exhaustion, anesthesia, or other situational constraints. Excruciating attribution therefore remains an inferential judgment that must be made cautiously and on the basis of converging evidence.

**Methodological caution (source-stated).** The attribution of conditions to this category must be done cautiously. Excruciating is reserved for intensity profiles supported by converging evidence and should not be applied loosely.

## 9. Adjacent-category distinctions

The following criteria are drawn from the source's category matrix and are intended to make adjacent-level transitions inspectable. They are descriptive aids, not exhaustive decision rules.

**No Pain ↔ Annoying.**
- No Pain: no negative Affective State hypothesized.
- Annoying: Pain is perceptible to the individual but ignorable most of the time; no measurable neurophysiological departures; no overt expression expected; motivated and positive behaviors are not deterred.

**Annoying ↔ Hurtful.**
- Annoying: attention can be drawn away from Pain most of the time; positive activities with no immediate function are not deterred; analgesics or psychotropics not expected to have effect.
- Hurtful: ability to draw attention away from Pain is reduced; motivated behaviors continue but with reduced frequency or duration; engagement in positive activities with no immediate benefit is not expected; typical drug doses are expected to be effective; mild neurophysiological departures appear.

**Hurtful ↔ Disabling.**
- Hurtful: routine activities continue with impairment; risk-taking behavior is not affected; drug response at typical doses.
- Disabling: Pain takes priority over most behavioral bids; positive welfare is prevented; willingness to trade important resources, work, and safety to avoid Pain; relief often requires higher doses or more powerful drugs; marked neurophysiological departures. The prey-species expression caveat (§7) is most consequential at this transition.

**Disabling ↔ Excruciating.**
- Disabling: continuous distress with substantial — but not total — disruption of activity and attention; some residual cognitive and behavioral function remains.
- Excruciating: not normally tolerated for more than seconds; behaviors that threaten body integrity may appear; cognitive functioning collapses; concealment is not possible; relief may require anesthesia or unconsciousness.

## 10. Inferential cautions and limitations

1. **Overt expression vs. functional disruption vs. inferred intensity.** These three constructs must be kept distinct (see §2.8 and §7). Inferred affective intensity is the welfare-relevant target. Overt expression and functional disruption are two among several streams of evidence used to infer that target.

2. **Absence of overt expression is not absence of Pain.** Especially in prey species, in concealment-adapted species, in early-life stages, and in contexts where expression is suppressed (restraint, immobility, exhaustion, anesthetic agents lacking analgesic action, social inhibition), conspicuous behavioral signs may be absent even at high inferred intensities.

3. **Stimulus magnitude is not a substitute for intensity inference.** A more severe Biological Consequence may increase the probability of higher-intensity Pain, but this relationship must be assessed against the converging-evidence base, not assumed.

4. **Inferred intensity is segment-specific.** The same condition may produce different intensity profiles across temporal segments, individuals, modulating circumstances, and treatment statuses. Single-point classification of an entire condition is incompatible with the segment-based logic of Pain-Tracks.

5. **Drug responsiveness is one evidence stream among several.** The pharmacological criteria embedded in each category description are informative but not dispositive. Failure of typical analgesic doses may reflect intensity but may also reflect drug-pharmacokinetic, mechanism-of-action, or administration factors. Drug response should be triangulated against the other evidence streams.

6. **Species-specific operationalization is not fully resolved.** The source provides qualitative criteria largely informed by mammalian evidence. Operationalization for non-mammalian taxa — including birds, fish, cephalopods, decapods, and other animals for which sentience is plausible — requires further work. Application to such taxa should preserve the conceptual definitions while explicitly flagging which descriptors are well-supported and which are extrapolated.

7. **Category boundaries are not numerically defined.** No numeric thresholds, weights, or equivalences are asserted in this file. Adjacent categories are qualitatively distinct, and their ordering does not imply equal intervals.

8. **No Pain ≠ no welfare event.** A segment classified as No Pain may still contain positive Affective Experience. The two valences are tracked independently.

## 11. Visual encoding — Pain intensity color gradient

The following hex codes are the **definitive WFF visual-encoding colors** for the four active Pain intensity categories. They are used in WFF visual tools, including the Pain-Track, in dashboards, and in published Welfare Footprint outputs.

| Category | Hex code | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Annoying | `#939238` | muted olive/yellow |
| Hurtful | `#B8923B` | ochre / amber-brown |
| Disabling | `#B53F2F` | burnt red |
| Excruciating | `#7A3A5F` | dark plum / deep suffering tone |

**Gradient character.** The Pain color gradient is warm, ordered from lower to higher Pain intensity. The progression Annoying → Hurtful → Disabling → Excruciating moves from muted olive/yellow through ochre into burnt red and ends in dark plum, providing a perceptually ordered visual cue for the increasing severity of negative affective states.

**No Pain.** No Pain is not assigned a Pain-side color in this gradient. Visual representations of Pain-Tracks should render No-Pain segments in a neutral or background style (for example, an unfilled cell or a neutral gray) so that No-Pain segments remain visible in temporal accounting without being confused with active Pain intensity categories.

**Status of the encoding.**

- The color mappings above are canonical WFF visualization conventions. They are not provisional, deferred, or pending transcription.
- Their formal integration into a machine-readable canon artifact is now implemented as `visualization/intensity_colors.yaml`, a derived mirror of these mappings for use by downstream WFF tools; this implementation is recorded in FU-004 (see §12 below).

**Methodological constraints (carried forward).** The color encoding is a visual convention for human-readable rendering of Pain intensity. It does not modify, and must not be read as modifying, the methodological rules established elsewhere in this file:

- Color coding does **not** introduce numeric weights, ratios, or arithmetic equivalences between Pain intensity categories.
- Adjacent colors do **not** imply equal perceptual or affective intervals between adjacent intensity categories.
- Cumulative Pain remains disaggregated by intensity category and is reported as time within each named category, not as a single colored aggregate.
- Pain colors are valence-specific. Pain and Pleasure color gradients are not compared in this file; cross-valence visual comparison must follow the rules in `canon/terminology.md`.
- Color assignment is fixed per category. Within-category gradients, blends, or alpha-modulated shading must not be used to imply sub-category numeric intensity.

## 12. Open questions

The following items are tracked for follow-up, not blockers for this file's review:

1. **Non-mammalian operationalization.** How the operational descriptors apply to birds, fish, cephalopods, decapods, and other plausibly sentient taxa for which behavioral, neurophysiological, and pharmacological evidence streams differ from the mammalian default. Cross-reference to be added in `review/open_questions.md` once that file is populated.
2. **Body-integrity-threatening behaviors in non-human animals.** The Excruciating criterion concerning behaviors the individual would normally refrain from is well-defined in humans but requires careful operationalization in other species, including specification of what counts as a normally-avoided body-integrity-threatening behavior for the species in question.
3. **Color-code registry integration.** Canonical WFF visualization color codes for Pain and Pleasure intensity categories are defined (see §11 for the Pain gradient). Their formal integration into a machine-readable canon artifact is now implemented as `visualization/intensity_colors.yaml` (a derived mirror referenced by downstream WFF tools and by the Welfare Footprint Atlas), with the conventions documented in `canon/visualization_conventions.md`; this implementation/resolution is recorded in FU-004. These color codes remain visualization conventions, not part of the scientific definitions of the intensity categories.
4. **Glossary expansion.** Whether `glossary/wff_terms.yaml` should gain per-category entries (`no_pain`, `annoying`, `hurtful`, `disabling`, `excruciating`) is deferred to a later reviewed PR, symmetrically with the eventual Pleasure-side categories. The existing `pain_intensity` and `cumulative_pain` entries already reference the category set collectively.
5. **Concurrent Affective Experiences.** Attention-related effects of concurrent negative and positive Affective Experiences at different intensities are addressed at the metric level (Cumulative Affect [Attention-Adjusted]); their interaction with intensity classification at the segment level remains open.
