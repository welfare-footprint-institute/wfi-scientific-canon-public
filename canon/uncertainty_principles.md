## Core commitment

WFI makes uncertainty explicit. No estimate is presented without a corresponding
account of its uncertainty. Where formal quantification is not yet possible,
the nature and direction of uncertainty must be described qualitatively.

---

## Types of uncertainty recognised in WFF

### Aleatory uncertainty
Natural variability in the biological system — e.g., individual variation in
pain sensitivity, herd-level variation in disease prevalence. Cannot be
eliminated by more research; must be characterised and propagated.

### Epistemic uncertainty
Uncertainty arising from incomplete knowledge — e.g., limited epidemiological
data, absence of studies in a specific species or system. Can in principle be
reduced by further research.

### Model uncertainty
Uncertainty arising from the choices made in the WFF methodology itself —
e.g., intensity category boundaries, weighting schemes in Module V,
interspecific scaling factors in Module Ψ. Must be documented separately
from empirical uncertainty.

---

## Rules for handling uncertainty

1. **Do not suppress uncertainty.** An estimate with wide bounds is more
   scientifically honest than a point estimate presented without qualification.

2. **Distinguish types.** Separate aleatory, epistemic, and model uncertainty
   where possible.

3. **Propagate uncertainty.** When combining estimates across modules, propagate
   uncertainty rather than carrying only point estimates forward.

4. **Document assumptions directionally.** Where a precise uncertainty bound
   is not available, document the likely direction of the assumption's effect
   on the final estimate (conservative, central, liberal).

5. **Do not treat open questions as resolved.** Open questions in `review/`
   represent genuine scientific uncertainty. They are not to be silently closed
   by adding content to canon files.
