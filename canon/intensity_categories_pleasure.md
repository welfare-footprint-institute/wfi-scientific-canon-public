## 1. Conceptual overview

This file provides the operational definitions of the WFF Pleasure intensity categories. It complements `canon/terminology.md`, which establishes the surrounding terms (Pleasure, Pleasure Intensity, Affective State, Pleasure-Track, Cumulative Pleasure) but defers the full per-category definitions to this file.

In the WFF, **Pleasure** is the umbrella term for any felt positive affective state. The primary source defines Pleasure as a conscious experience evolved to elicit or reinforce behaviors beneficial to an organism's survival and/or reproduction, processed as a positive and dynamic sensation that can vary in intensity, duration, texture, spatial specificity, and anatomical location. The source further distinguishes "physical" Pleasure, primarily triggered by directly rewarding or enjoyable stimuli, from "psychological" Pleasure, triggered by cognitive processes, memories, and emotional states. Depending on intensity and duration, Pleasure can also override other adaptive instincts and motivational drives, leading to states of dependency and self-damage.

**Pleasure Intensity** denotes the subjective degree of engagement with positive experiences. The primary source operationally defines Pleasure intensity categories predominantly by the **degree of engagement with positive experiences**. This is the central definitional axis on the Pleasure side, and it is structurally different from the Pain-side axis of unpleasantness, disruption, and attention capture (see §3 and §10). Pleasure intensity is not inferred directly from the magnitude of a triggering stimulus, nor from the adaptive value of the activity in isolation; it is a hypothesis about the felt positive engagement of the experience itself.

Pleasure intensity categories are **assigned to temporal segments within an Affective Experience, not globally to an animal, a condition, or an activity**. The same activity may give rise to different Pleasure intensities at different segments of its temporal course, in different individuals, or under different modulating circumstances. The Pleasure-Track illustration in the primary source (a hypothetical play bout in young calves, decomposed into five sequential phases of different intensities) exemplifies this segment-level logic.

Pleasure intensity categories are **hypotheses about positive affective experience inferred from converging streams of evidence** — behavioral, physiological, motivational, contextual, and (where applicable) neurophysiological and evolutionary. They are revisable in the light of new evidence.

## 2. Methodological principles

The following principles govern the use of Pleasure intensity categories throughout the WFF and apply to every per-category definition below.

1. **Subjective, not stimulus-based.** Intensity refers to the felt positive experience and the degree of engagement with it, not to the physical magnitude of the triggering stimulus nor to the adaptive value of the activity in isolation.
2. **Segment-level, not global.** Categories are assigned to temporal segments of Affective Experiences. An individual is not classified as "a Joy animal"; a segment of an Affective Experience is classified as Joy.
3. **Hypothesis-based, evidence-informed.** Each intensity assignment in the WFF is an explicit inferential hypothesis about the most plausible positive affective intensity associated with an Affective Experience, grounded in converging welfare indicators such as the vigor and persistence of engagement, motivational behavior, physiological correlates (where available), contextual evidence about the kind of experience involved, and any further species-relevant streams.

   Because Affective Experiences cannot be directly observed, the framework does not treat intensity assignment as a deterministic classification. Instead, available evidence is used to estimate the relative plausibility that an experience falls within different intensity categories. Stronger, more consistent, and more directly relevant evidence should reduce uncertainty, typically concentrating estimates within one or a narrower set of adjacent intensity categories. Weaker, conflicting, indirect, or sparse evidence should produce broader plausibility distributions across categories, with assumptions and uncertainty made explicit. The primary source itself emphasizes that, in real applications, numerical inputs to a Pleasure-Track should be based on a thorough review of evidence and that criticism of the proposed values should always be encouraged.

   This approach is consistent with the WFF distinction between welfare indicators, which provide indirect evidence about affective states, and welfare metrics, which integrate such evidence into structured estimates of time spent in Pleasure or Pain.
4. **Disaggregated.** Cumulative Pleasure remains disaggregated by intensity category. Time spent in Satisfaction, Joy, Euphoria, and Bliss is reported separately. Categories are not collapsed into a single Pleasure scale, mirroring the source's stated position that no empirical references currently support a weighting system across the four Pleasure intensity categories.
5. **No numeric weights are defined in this file.** This file does not assign numerical weights, ratios, or equivalences between intensity categories. Any such weighting belongs to optional analytical layers and must be documented with explicit assumptions where used.
6. **No cross-category equivalence is implied.** Adjacent categories are qualitatively distinct. The category ordering reflects increasing engagement with positive experience but does not imply equal spacing or arithmetic relationships between adjacent levels.
7. **No cross-valence comparison here.** Pleasure intensity categories are not compared with Pain intensity categories in this file. Pain and Pleasure are valence-specific, are not assumed to sit on a common scale, and are not netted against each other. The primary source explicitly avoids equating the intensity categories of Pleasure and Pain and explicitly questions whether, for example, ten seconds of Bliss can be considered a direct counterpart of ten seconds of Excruciating Pain. See `canon/terminology.md` and §10 below.
8. **Absence of overt positive expression is not, by itself, absence of Pleasure.** Three constructs must remain distinct: (a) the **inferred positive affective intensity** of the felt experience, (b) the **functional engagement** observable in motivated and exploratory behavior, and (c) the **overt expression of Pleasure** through vocalizations, postures, exuberant body movement, social affiliative signals, or similar manifestations. Overt positive expression is one stream of evidence among many; its absence should not by itself be treated as absence of Pleasure. This safeguard is articulated here in a Pleasure-specific form; it does not transfer unchanged from the Pain-side prey-species caveat, whose evolutionary rationale (concealment of weakness from predators) is not symmetric on the Pleasure side.
9. **Maladaptive Pleasure is acknowledged but not operationalized here.** The primary source explicitly notes that the link between intensity of engagement and adaptive value admits maladaptive exceptions, citing addiction in humans and feather-plucking in birds. Such cases are recognized as real but are flagged as a limitation in §12 (Open Questions) rather than operationalized inside the per-category definitions below.

## 3. Canonical categories

The WFF Pleasure intensity categories, in canonical order, are:

1. **No Pleasure**
2. **Satisfaction**
3. **Joy**
4. **Euphoria**
5. **Bliss**

**No Pleasure** is not an active positive Pleasure intensity category and does not contribute to Cumulative Pleasure totals. It denotes periods in which no positive affective state categorized as Pleasure is hypothesized to be experienced. It remains an explicit state within Pleasure-Track segmentation and temporal accounting, so that segments without Pleasure are represented explicitly rather than implicitly. The primary source introduces No Pleasure in direct parallel to No Pain, as a category to be used whenever relevant to represent the hypothesis that no positive affective states are experienced over the period of interest.

The four active positive intensity categories, ordered by increasing engagement with positive experience, are: **Satisfaction → Joy → Euphoria → Bliss**.

**Note on the definitional axis.** This ordering reflects the primary source's stated criterion: Pleasure intensity is defined predominantly by the degree of engagement with positive experiences. It is **not** simply the inverse of the Pain-side definitional axis (unpleasantness, disruption, attention capture). The engagement axis is treated explicitly here so that downstream readers and tools do not silently mirror Pain-side mechanics onto Pleasure-side mechanics (see §10 for the full asymmetry summary).

## 4. No Pleasure

**Definition.** A temporal segment in which no positive affective state categorized as Pleasure is hypothesized to be experienced.

**Role in Pleasure-Track segmentation.** No Pleasure is a legitimate state within the Pleasure-Track state-space. Segments classified as No Pleasure are explicitly represented in temporal accounting so that the absence of positive affect during a portion of an Affective Experience is recorded rather than left implicit.

**Role in Cumulative Pleasure.** No Pleasure segments do not contribute to time in Satisfaction, Joy, Euphoria, or Bliss. They are tracked separately for completeness of temporal accounting.

**Relationship to Pain.** A segment classified as No Pleasure is silent only about Pleasure. Whether the same segment contains negative Affective Experience is a separate determination made within the Pain-Track and Cumulative Pain constructs.

**Source basis.** Derived from `wfi-positive-welfare-2024`, which explicitly introduces a "no pleasure" category, used whenever relevant, to represent the hypothesis that no positive affective states are experienced over the period of interest. Supported by `canon/terminology.md` (Pleasure, Pleasure Intensity, Affective State entries) and `wff-methodological-foundations-2025`.

## 5. Satisfaction

**Summary.** Low-intensity positive states in which the individual shows subtle signs of comfort or satisfaction associated with a physical sensation or with the meeting of a non-essential need.

**Operational descriptors (from `wfi-positive-welfare-2024`):**

- **Character of the state:** low-intensity positive affect; subtle signs of comfort or satisfaction.
- **Typical triggering contexts:** physical sensations or the meeting of non-essential needs. Source examples include comfortable bedding conditions, grooming, and basking in the sun.
- **Engagement:** present but not overwhelming. The individual can easily shift attention to other stimuli or activities as needed.
- **Attention to surroundings:** preserved. The individual remains aware of, and responsive to, other stimuli.
- **Overt expression of Pleasure:** subtle; not prominent.
- **Physiological correlates:** not emphasized at this level in the source.

**Distinguishing character.** Positive affect is present and detectable through subtle behavioral and contextual cues, but the engagement is light enough that the individual remains free to redirect attention to other stimuli or activities. The state does not preempt other behaviors.

---

## 6. Joy

**Summary.** Positive states involving greater engagement in rewarding activities, with behavior noticeably directed toward maintaining or enhancing the positive experience, yet without that experience exclusively dominating the individual's attention.

**Operational descriptors (from `wfi-positive-welfare-2024`):**

- **Character of the state:** moderately intense positive affect with greater engagement than Satisfaction.
- **Typical behavioral manifestations:** enhanced vigor in play, stronger social bonding, more active engagement in highly preferred activities such as foraging.
- **Direction of behavior:** noticeably directed toward maintaining or enhancing the positive experience.
- **Focus:** greater focus on the positive experience, while still not exclusively dominating attention.
- **Physiological correlates:** heightened autonomic responses may be present (e.g., heart rate), per the source.
- **Overt expression of Pleasure:** more visible than at Satisfaction, including more energetic engagement and social affiliative behaviors; not yet at the level of spontaneous, expressive vocalizations characteristic of Euphoria.

**Distinguishing character.** The individual is now visibly motivated to maintain or enhance the positive experience, but the experience does not exclude responsiveness to other relevant stimuli.

---

## 7. Euphoria

**Summary.** Intense positive states in which the positive experience becomes the primary focus of attention, with everything else seeming secondary for the duration of the experience.

**Operational descriptors (from `wfi-positive-welfare-2024`):**

- **Character of the state:** intense positive affect; the positive experience is the primary focus of attention.
- **Typical contexts:** immersive play, mating rituals, and the pursuit and enjoyment of highly favored resources. The primary source supplies a successful hunt as an illustrative example of such contexts; this is a source example, not a WFI normative endorsement of any particular activity.
- **Focus:** other stimuli and activities tend to seem secondary while the state is active.
- **Overt expression of Pleasure:** in some situations, this intense state may lead to spontaneous expressions of Pleasure, such as vocalizations.
- **Physiological correlates:** further heightened relative to Joy where available; not exhaustively specified by the source.

**Distinguishing character.** The shift from Joy to Euphoria is characterized by the positive experience becoming the primary, rather than merely the directed, focus of attention. Spontaneous expressive behavior may, but need not, appear (see §10 on the Pleasure-side expression safeguard).

---

## 8. Bliss

**Summary.** A profound positive state that pervades sensory and emotional experience and, for its duration, eclipses other immediate needs and environmental stimuli. The source describes Bliss as expected to be rare and of short duration.

**Operational descriptors (from `wfi-positive-welfare-2024`):**

- **Character of the state:** profound positive affect; pleasure pervades the individual's sensory and emotional experience and transcends ordinary positive states.
- **Effect on attention to other needs and stimuli:** the degree of positive engagement is so intense that other stimuli and immediate needs are strongly backgrounded for the duration of the experience. The primary source describes this state as one in which the individual is consumed by an all-encompassing sensation, eclipsing other immediate needs or environmental stimuli.
- **Typical contexts (source examples):** orgasmic states, reuniting with socially significant partners after prolonged separation, or the encounter of extremely positive conditions after prolonged periods of stress and hardship.
- **Source-stated temporal expectation:** Blissful states are expected to be rare and of short duration.
- **Overt expression of Pleasure:** may be highly expressive, but its presence or absence does not, by itself, settle the inferential question (see §2.8 and §10).

**Temporal-expectation caution.** The source-stated rarity and short duration of Bliss is an expectation about how this state is typically realized, not a derived methodological rule about the temporal limits of high-intensity affect in general. It should not be mechanically mirrored onto the Pain side; the Pain-side category Excruciating has its own source-stated temporal characterization (not normally tolerated for more than seconds, per `wfi-pain-intensities`), and the two should not be conflated. The structural asymmetry between Bliss and Excruciating is treated in §10.

**Methodological caution.** Attribution of segments to Bliss must be made cautiously. Bliss should be reserved for positive intensity profiles supported by converging evidence, consistent with the source-stated rarity of the state.

## 9. Adjacent-category distinctions

The following criteria are derived from the source's narrative descriptors and are intended to make adjacent-level transitions inspectable. They are descriptive aids, not exhaustive decision rules.

**No Pleasure ↔ Satisfaction.**
- No Pleasure: no positive Affective State hypothesized.
- Satisfaction: subtle signs of comfort or contentment are present; engagement is light and easily redirected; the individual remains attentive to surroundings.

**Satisfaction ↔ Joy.**
- Satisfaction: engagement is present but not overwhelming; attention is easily shifted; behavior is not specifically directed toward maintaining or enhancing the positive state.
- Joy: engagement is greater; behavior is noticeably directed toward maintaining or enhancing the positive experience; vigor in play, stronger social bonding, or more active engagement in preferred activities becomes characteristic; the positive experience receives greater focus but does not yet dominate attention.

**Joy ↔ Euphoria.**
- Joy: behavior is directed toward, but does not exclusively focus on, the positive experience; the individual remains responsive to other relevant stimuli.
- Euphoria: the positive experience becomes the primary focus of attention; other stimuli and activities tend to seem secondary for the duration of the state; spontaneous expressions of Pleasure (e.g., vocalizations) may, but need not, appear.

**Euphoria ↔ Bliss.**
- Euphoria: the positive experience is the primary focus, but other stimuli and immediate needs are not yet strongly backgrounded in the way characteristic of Bliss.
- Bliss: positive affect is so profound that it eclipses other immediate needs and environmental stimuli; the experience pervades sensory and emotional experience. The source's stated expectation that Bliss is rare and of short duration is a characteristic feature of this transition.

## 10. Asymmetries with Pain (must not be silently mirrored)

This section is deliberately included so that downstream readers and tools do not treat the Pleasure intensity scale as the inverse of the Pain intensity scale. The Pleasure and Pain categories are valence-specific, are defined on structurally different axes, and must not be netted, equated, or reflected onto each other.

1. **Definitional axes differ.** Pain intensity categories in the WFF are defined predominantly by unpleasantness, disruption of motivated behavior, and attention capture. Pleasure intensity categories are defined predominantly by the degree of engagement with positive experiences. The Pleasure scale is therefore not the negative of the Pain scale; it is a separate scale on a separate axis.
2. **No common metric scale is implied by parallel ordering.** Listing four active Pleasure categories alongside four active Pain categories is a structural parallel for readability and analytical convenience. It does not imply that, for example, Joy is the positive-side mirror of Hurtful, nor that Bliss is the positive-side mirror of Excruciating.
3. **No netting across valences.** Cumulative Pleasure and Cumulative Pain are reported as time within categories per valence and are not netted. The primary source explicitly does not attempt to estimate how cumulative time in Pain and Pleasure might balance each other out.
4. **No equivalence of duration at extremes.** The primary source explicitly questions whether ten seconds of Bliss can be considered a direct counterpart of ten seconds of Excruciating Pain, and treats this as an open question rather than an established equivalence.
5. **The expression safeguard takes a softer form on the Pleasure side.** On the Pain side, the inferential safeguard "absence of overt expression is not absence of Pain" carries a specific evolutionary rationale (concealment of weakness, especially in prey species). On the Pleasure side, the safeguard is: *absence of overt positive expression should not by itself be treated as absence of Pleasure.* The Pleasure-side safeguard is real and important, but the Pain-side prey-species evolutionary rationale does not transfer unchanged to Pleasure; the asymmetry of the underlying evolutionary pressures should be preserved.
6. **Temporal expectations are not mirrored.** Bliss has a source-stated expectation of rarity and short duration. This should not be mechanically mirrored onto Pain categories. In particular, Excruciating Pain has its own source-stated temporal characterization (not normally tolerated for more than seconds, per `wfi-pain-intensities`), driven by physiological tolerance and behavioral collapse, not by parallel reasoning with Bliss. The two characterizations are independent.
7. **Negativity bias is acknowledged but does not collapse the scales.** The primary source acknowledges that negative experiences generally have a greater impact on well-being than positive ones (negativity bias, well-documented in humans) and that severe Pain can prevent the possibility of positive experiences. This recognition is preserved as a framework-level consideration; it does not authorize any arithmetic netting of categories across valences.

## 11. Visual encoding — Pleasure intensity color gradient

The following hex codes are the **definitive WFF visual-encoding colors** for the four active Pleasure intensity categories. They are used in WFF visual tools, including the Pleasure-Track, in dashboards, and in published Welfare Footprint outputs.

| Category | Hex code | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Satisfaction | `#ADD8E6` | light blue |
| Joy | `#40E0D0` | turquoise |
| Euphoria | `#008080` | teal |
| Bliss | `#483D8B` | deep indigo |

**Gradient character.** The Pleasure color gradient is cool, ordered from lower to higher Pleasure intensity. The progression Satisfaction → Joy → Euphoria → Bliss moves from light blue through turquoise into teal and ends in deep indigo, providing a perceptually ordered visual cue for the increasing engagement with positive affective states.

**No Pleasure.** No Pleasure is not assigned a Pleasure-side color in this gradient. Visual representations of Pleasure-Tracks should render No-Pleasure segments in a neutral or background style (for example, an unfilled cell or a neutral gray) so that No-Pleasure segments remain visible in temporal accounting without being confused with active Pleasure intensity categories.

**Status of the encoding.**

- The color mappings above are canonical WFF visualization conventions. They are not provisional, deferred, or pending transcription.
- Their formal integration into a machine-readable canon artifact is now implemented as `visualization/intensity_colors.yaml`, a derived mirror of these mappings for use by downstream WFF tools; this implementation is recorded in FU-004 (see §12 below).

**Methodological constraints (carried forward).** The color encoding is a visual convention for human-readable rendering of Pleasure intensity. It does not modify, and must not be read as modifying, the methodological rules established elsewhere in this file:

- Color coding does **not** introduce numeric weights, ratios, or arithmetic equivalences between Pleasure intensity categories.
- Adjacent colors do **not** imply equal perceptual or affective intervals between adjacent intensity categories.
- Cumulative Pleasure remains disaggregated by intensity category and is reported as time within each named category, not as a single colored aggregate.
- Pleasure colors are valence-specific. Pleasure and Pain color gradients are not compared in this file; cross-valence visual comparison must follow the rules in `canon/terminology.md` and the asymmetry rules in §10.
- Color assignment is fixed per category. Within-category gradients, blends, or alpha-modulated shading must not be used to imply sub-category numeric intensity.

## 12. Open questions

The following items are tracked for follow-up, not blockers for this file's review:

1. **Non-mammalian operationalization.** How the operational descriptors apply to birds, fish, cephalopods, decapods, and other plausibly sentient taxa for which behavioral, physiological, and contextual evidence streams differ from the mammalian default. Cross-reference to be added in `review/open_questions.md` once that file is populated.
2. **Maladaptive Pleasure.** The primary source notes that high engagement with positive experiences admits maladaptive exceptions, citing addiction in humans and feather-plucking in birds. The classification of such states within the four-category Pleasure scale — and whether they warrant any additional analytical annotation distinct from the intensity assignment itself — remains an open methodological question. This file does not operationalize such states within the per-category definitions; treatment is deferred to a future reviewed PR.
3. **Cross-valence equivalence questions raised in the source.** The primary source explicitly raises, as an open question, whether (for example) ten seconds of Bliss can be considered a direct counterpart of ten seconds of Excruciating Pain. This file preserves that question as open and does not assert any such equivalence.
4. **Color-code registry integration.** Canonical WFF visualization color codes for Pleasure and Pain intensity categories are defined (see §11 for the Pleasure gradient, and §11 of `canon/intensity_categories_pain.md` for the Pain gradient). Their formal integration into a machine-readable canon artifact is now implemented as `visualization/intensity_colors.yaml` (a derived mirror referenced by downstream WFF tools and by the Welfare Footprint Atlas), with the conventions documented in `canon/visualization_conventions.md`; this implementation/resolution is recorded in FU-004. These color codes remain visualization conventions, not part of the scientific definitions of the intensity categories.
5. **Glossary expansion.** Whether `glossary/wff_terms.yaml` should gain per-category entries (`no_pleasure`, `satisfaction`, `joy`, `euphoria`, `bliss`) is deferred to a later reviewed PR, symmetrically with the Pain-side categories. The existing `pleasure_intensity` and `cumulative_pleasure` entries already reference the category set collectively.
6. **Concurrent Affective Experiences.** Attention-related effects of concurrent positive and negative Affective Experiences at different intensities are addressed at the metric level (Cumulative Affect [Attention-Adjusted]); their interaction with intensity classification at the segment level remains open.
